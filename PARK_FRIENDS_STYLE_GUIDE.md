# PARK FRIENDS Style Guide

## Story Spine

The park is not haunted in a generic way. It is waking up.

Construction north of Bellwether Park has disturbed something old beneath the roots and concrete. The animals are acting in patterns because the park is trying to defend itself without understanding people, traffic, paperwork, or machines. The player is not trying to "defeat evil." The player is trying to understand what the park wants before the city hurts it by accident.

The emotional rule:

- Every funny moment should sit next to something slightly sad or off.
- Every clue should feel local and physical.
- Every area should imply a human history before the player arrived.

## First Playable Slice

This is the minimum slice that should always feel polished:

1. Shelly starts at the park entrance.
2. The ranger establishes that the park has become weird in a specific, bureaucratic way.
3. The fountain plaza introduces the first problem.
4. Hazel gives the player a concrete, funny, emotionally grounded task.
5. A pigeon battle proves the game loop.
6. The returned item pays off with party growth and a new route.

Objective copy should always be explicit. The player should never need to guess the next action.

## Area Craft Rules

Every chunk needs:

- 1 landmark
- 1 small joke
- 1 sign of human maintenance
- 1 sign that maintenance is losing
- 1 object that implies recent animal interference
- 1 readable exit

Example:

- A bench is better than an empty tile.
- A bench with a missing slat is better than a bench.
- A bench with a missing slat, a tipped trash can, and a sign nobody obeys feels like Bellwether Park.

## Visual Rules

- Favor chunky silhouettes over detail.
- No emoji-looking symmetry.
- Highlights come from the upper-left.
- Shadows sit lower-right and use palette tint, not neutral gray.
- UI should read like a handheld/SNES menu, not iOS chrome.
- Props should look installed, weathered, or slightly knocked out of alignment.

## UI Rules

- Use boxed panels with strong borders.
- Prefer monospaced or blocky text treatment.
- Use cursor language, labels, and status readouts instead of glossy buttons.
- Keep interface colors limited: navy, bone, grass, gold, warning red.

## Tone Rules

- Dialogue is short and specific.
- Jokes come from observation, not random absurdity.
- Bureaucratic language is funnier when played straight.
- Even enemies should feel like park-world problems, not generic RPG monsters.
